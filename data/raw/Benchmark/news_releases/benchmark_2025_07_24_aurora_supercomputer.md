## Summary

Benchmark announced the successful commissioning and validation of the Aurora exascale supercomputer at Argonne National Laboratory, completing its role in manufacturing, test development, and test support for Intel. Aurora is capable of performing one quintillion calculations per second (1 exaFLOPS) and will primarily serve the U.S. scientific community and the Department of Energy (DOE).

## Technical Details

### Liquid Cooling

Aurora uses liquid cooling to manage the extreme power density of exascale computing. Benchmark's expertise in liquid-cooled HPC subsystems was critical to the project's success.

**Liquid Cooling Benefits:**
- Higher power density support
- Better thermal performance for sustained performance
- Energy efficiency (lower PUE)
- Enables exascale performance in reasonable footprint

### System Architecture

**Intel Technology:**
- Intel Xeon CPUs (Sapphire Rapids)
- Intel Data Center GPU Max Series (Ponte Vecchio)
- Intel Optane persistent memory
- HPE Cray EX supercomputer architecture
- HPE Slingshot interconnect

**Scale:**
- 10,000+ compute nodes
- 60,000+ Intel GPUs
- Liquid-cooled infrastructure
- Exascale performance (1+ exaFLOPS)

## Argonne Leadership Computing Facility Legacy

### Supercomputer History

**Previous Systems:**
- **Intrepid:** IBM Blue Gene/P (2008-2015)
- **Mira:** IBM Blue Gene/Q (2012-2019)
- **Theta:** Cray XC40 (2017-2024)
- **Polaris:** HPE Apollo 6500 Gen10+ (2022-present, testbed for Aurora)
- **Aurora:** HPE Cray EX (2025-present, exascale)

**Evolution:**
- Petascale (Intrepid, Mira) → Pre-exascale (Theta, Polaris) → Exascale (Aurora)
- CPU-centric → GPU-accelerated → AI-integrated
- Air-cooled → Liquid-cooled
- Simulation-focused → AI + Simulation + Data
